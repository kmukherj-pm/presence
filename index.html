The problem
"I commuted in on Tuesday and spent the whole day on Zoom. I didn't see a single person I actually needed to talk to."
Hybrid work promised flexibility. What it quietly delivered was a new kind of loneliness — the empty office commute. You go in hoping for human contact. You arrive to a half-empty floor and back-to-back video calls. The colleagues you needed were home that day. You had no way of knowing.
Envoy already holds the most valuable piece of data in this puzzle: real, verified check-in history for every employee. Not calendar intentions — actual behavioral patterns, timestamped, per person. This prototype surfaces that data as a daily briefing: who's predicted to be in, who you haven't seen in weeks, which desk puts you near your collaborators, and whether it's worth the commute at all.
The hard product problem isn't the data. It's the editorial layer — deciding which of dozens of true signals is worth surfacing on a given morning. That's an AI judgment problem, not a dashboard problem. And it's the difference between a tool people open once and one they reach for before they've had coffee.

The connecting thread
Proximity without connection is just
expensive real estate.
We've built remarkable infrastructure for managing spaces — access control, occupancy analytics, visitor management. What we haven't built is infrastructure for the human layer on top. These prototypes argue that the platforms closest to where people physically are — their office building and their home building — are the most natural place for that layer to live.

The problem
"I've lived in this building for two years. I know my neighbour's dog's name but not their name."
Apartment buildings are among the densest concentrations of people in modern life — and among the loneliest places to live. The architecture optimises for privacy and efficiency. Nobody designed it for connection. You share walls, elevators, and amenity spaces with dozens of people, and interact with almost none of them.
ButterflyMX already manages access to the building, the amenity spaces, and the mailroom. It knows — at a building level — who is home, what spaces are available, and what's happening in the community. What it doesn't yet do is surface that intelligence to residents in a way that makes connection feel effortless rather than awkward.
This prototype is a morning briefing for your home: which neighbours are working from home today and might want company for lunch, what amenity spaces are free, what community events are coming up, and — crucially — one-tap access to everything ButterflyMX already controls. The door to the mailroom. The front lobby. A guest pass for a visitor. All from the same surface that greeted you this morning.
